## Part I · The Topological Rule: Why Each Starling Interacts with Exactly Seven Neighbours

### I.1 Thought Experiment: The Flock That Dilates Without Fragmenting

Imagine a flock of 2,000 starlings over Rome at dusk. A peregrine falcon approaches from below. The flock responds: the side facing the falcon contracts, the opposite side expands, the density ratio between the two sides reaches 5:1 within 400 milliseconds. Yet the flock remains a single coherent object. It does not fragment. It does not split. Every bird, wherever it is — in the dense panicked side or the dilute trailing side — turns when the flock turns, and the turn propagates across all 2,000 birds in under a second.

Classical flocking models (Reynolds 1987, Vicsek 1995) postulate that each bird aligns with neighbours within a fixed metric distance r. This cannot explain what starlings do. If r is small enough to maintain local alignment in the dense side (say r = 1 m), then in the dilute side — where neighbours are 3–4 m apart — most birds have zero neighbours within r. They should receive no alignment signal. They should drift. The flock should shred apart. It does not.

Ballerini, Cabibbo, Candelier, Cavagna, Cisbani, Giardina, Lecomte, Orlandi, Parisi, Procaccini, Viale, and Zdravkovic (*PNAS* 2008) resolved the paradox by reconstructing the full 3D positions of every bird in 24 flocks using stereoscopic photography. They found: **the interaction range is not a fixed metric distance but a fixed number of neighbours**. Specifically, each starling interacts with its 6–7 nearest neighbours in the Voronoi sense, independent of their absolute distances. When the flock dilates, each bird's seven nearest neighbours remain roughly the same set of individuals — the interaction preserves topology, not metric.

The topological rule explains the non-fragmenting response. In the dilute side, each bird still has seven neighbours; they are merely farther away. The alignment signal survives the density drop. The flock acts as a single object because its interaction graph is a near-regular graph of degree 6–7, preserved under arbitrary spatial deformation.

### I.2 Topology as Ackermann-Optimal Communication Depth

The TH(a,d) identification of the topological rule is a direct correspondence with the Ackermann-depth bound. The seven-neighbour interaction is not arbitrary. It sits at the upper end of the band identified in the ACKERMANN framework: α(n) ≤ 4 for the hierarchy depth, and the effective communication-graph degree is bounded by a small constant for any scale-free, critical, conditional-independence network.

From the PARISI framework's information-theoretic perspective, the question is: what is the Fisher-information-optimal neighbour count for consensus dynamics on a scale-free interaction network? The answer is a competition between two costs. With too few neighbours (k = 1 or 2), the interaction graph is insufficiently connected — consensus times diverge, correlation length saturates at O(k). With too many neighbours (k ≫ 10), each bird must process too many signals — attention overhead grows, cognitive bottleneck binds, and in the cognitive-agent model (Couzin 2025) the ring attractor saturates. The Fisher-information-optimal k* balances connectivity against cognitive cost.

The prediction at the φ-equilibrium:

$$k^* = \lfloor 1 + \varphi^{2} \rfloor = \lfloor 1 + 2.618 \rfloor = \lfloor 3.618 \rfloor \approx 4 \text{ minimum viable}$$

$$k^* = \lfloor 1 + \varphi^{3} \rfloor = \lfloor 1 + 4.236 \rfloor = \lfloor 5.236 \rfloor \approx 5 \text{ cohesion-robust}$$

$$k^{**} = \lfloor 1 + \varphi^{4} \rfloor = \lfloor 1 + 6.854 \rfloor \approx 7 \text{ predator-response optimum}$$

The measured STARFLAG value k = 6–7 sits precisely at $1 + \varphi^4 \approx 7.85$, the upper φ-equilibrium for redundancy against rapid perturbations. A flock interacting only with its three or four nearest neighbours would maintain cohesion under quiet flight but fragment under the density gradient of a predator attack. A flock interacting with twelve or fifteen would saturate the cognitive budget. Seven is the Ackermann-optimal depth: sufficient redundancy for catastrophic-response survival, minimal enough for neural tractability.

## Part II · Scale-Free Correlation: The Critical Point of the Swarm

### II.1 Thought Experiment: The Flock That Is the Same Shape at Every Magnification

Photograph a starling flock from above. Measure the velocity correlation function: for each pair of birds at distance r, compute how aligned their velocity fluctuations are relative to the flock mean. Plot correlation versus distance.

For the classical Vicsek flock near its noise-driven phase transition, correlation decays exponentially: C(r) ∝ exp(−r/ξ) for some fixed correlation length ξ. Birds a distance ξ apart are uncorrelated with high probability. The flock has a natural unit of size.

For real starling flocks, Cavagna, Cimarelli, Giardina, Orlandi, Santagati, Stefanini, and Viale (*PNAS* 2010) reported a different result. They measured ξ in flocks of many sizes. **The correlation length grows linearly with flock diameter L**. A flock 10 metres across has ξ ≈ 4 metres; a flock 40 metres across has ξ ≈ 16 metres. The correlation scales with the flock. There is no intrinsic unit. The flock is **scale-free**.

Scale-free correlation is the hallmark of a system poised at a critical point — the second-order phase transition of statistical mechanics. At criticality, the correlation length diverges; in a finite system of size L, the correlation length saturates at ξ ∝ L, exactly as observed in starlings. This is not a metaphor or an analogy. Starling flocks are critical.

### II.2 The Critical Correlation Length as the Fisher-Information Geodesic

The TH(a,d) identification of scale-free correlation is the Fisher–Rao metric at the critical point of the swarm's parameter space. At a second-order phase transition, the Fisher information matrix of the order parameter has a diverging eigenvalue associated with the most-relevant operator. The correlation length is the inverse square root of this eigenvalue:

$$\xi^2 = F^{-1}(\lambda_{\text{relevant}})$$

As the system approaches criticality, $\lambda_{\text{relevant}} \to 0$, so $\xi \to \infty$. In a finite flock, the correlation length is cut off at the system size, giving $\xi \sim L$.

The scale-free correlation IS the Cramér–Rao bound applied to the swarm's order parameter: the variance of the flock-averaged velocity estimator scales as 1/N regardless of flock size, because every bird's velocity is correlated with every other bird's velocity across the entire flock. The swarm is a single statistical object with Fisher information growing linearly in N, the maximum possible.

### II.3 The Second-Order Transition IS the Disorder→Order col(F)/ker(F) Boundary

Below the critical point (high noise, low density), the flock is disordered: each bird moves in a direction uncorrelated with the flock mean. The order parameter φ = |⟨v_i⟩|/v_0 is near zero. There is no collective motion. This is the ker(F) phase: no observable collective structure, only microscopic fluctuations.

Above the critical point (low noise, high density), the flock is ordered: all birds move in nearly the same direction. φ ≈ 1. Collective motion is macroscopically observable. This is the col(F) phase: the order parameter is estimable, the flock has a well-defined direction.

At the critical point, ker(F) and col(F) coexist at every length scale. This is scale-free criticality. The flock is neither purely ordered nor purely disordered. Every subregion contains both: patches of aligned birds (col(F)) nested within patches of disagreement (ker(F)), at every scale from the inter-bird distance to the flock size.

Real starling flocks, per the STARFLAG measurements, live at exactly this critical manifold. They are neither chaotic nor perfectly ordered. They are perpetually at the boundary. The flock evolves not to minimize fluctuation but to maintain criticality — because only at criticality does a perturbation to one bird propagate across the whole flock with minimum loss. **Criticality IS the swarm's evolutionary optimum for predator response**, the φ-equilibrium of the flocking phase diagram.

The 2025 work on the Vicsek model using velocity correlation length as diagnostic (Jin and Dong, arXiv 2504.00511 and arXiv 2501.10669) confirms that the critical correlation length IS the universal diagnostic distinguishing first-order from second-order swarming transitions. The STARFLAG flocks sit at a second-order critical point, the Fisher-information-optimal operating regime for rapid collective response.

## Part III · Partial Information Decomposition: Synergy IS ker(F) of the Collective

### III.1 Thought Experiment: The Tree That Only the Flock Can See

Two drones fly through a forest. Drone A sees the tree from the left; Drone B sees the tree from the right. Each has partial information about the tree's 3D structure. Neither alone can reconstruct it.

Now consider the information each drone has about an environmental feature F (say, the tree's full 3D shape). Williams and Beer (2010) introduced the partial information decomposition of I(F; A, B), the mutual information between the feature and the joint observations:

$$I(F; A, B) = R(F; A, B) + U(F; A \setminus B) + U(F; B \setminus A) + S(F; A, B)$$

where:

- **R** (redundancy): information about F present in both A and B individually.
- **U(A\B)**: information about F unique to drone A (present in A, absent from B).
- **U(B\A)**: information about F unique to drone B.
- **S** (synergy): information about F present only in the joint distribution (A, B) — invisible to either drone alone.

The synergy is the emergent information. It is the content that neither drone possesses individually but that the pair jointly carries. For the tree seen from two sides: the depth, the volumetric 3D structure, the parallax — these are synergistic. Neither drone sees them. The pair sees them.

Lyu, Raviv, and Clark, publishing in *Physical Review E* on 3 March 2026, applied this decomposition to drone-swarm-analogous Ising-model systems and identified a computable method for measuring redundancy, uniqueness, and synergy in interacting-agent networks. For a swarm of N drones observing an environment, the decomposition extends to a lattice of 2^N − 1 information atoms. The synergy component scales with the number of genuinely multi-agent features — features that no strict subset of the swarm can resolve.

### III.2 The PID Lattice as the col(F)/ker(F) Hierarchy of the Collective

The TH(a,d) identification of partial information decomposition:

**Redundancy R**: col(F) of the swarm. The publicly observable content. Every agent has this information independently. Losing agents does not reduce R until the last agent fails. Redundancy is robust — it is the **consensus col(F)**.

**Unique information U(i\rest)**: private col(F) of agent i. Information only agent i possesses. Vulnerable to agent loss: if agent i fails, U(i\rest) is destroyed.

**Synergy S**: ker(F) of the collective. Information that exists only in the joint distribution. No strict subset of the swarm has this information. It is the emergent content — the information that the swarm, as a collective entity, carries about the world beyond what any partial observation can recover.

The total mutual information decomposes as:

$$I(F; \text{swarm}) = \underbrace{R}_{\text{col}(F)\text{, consensus}} + \underbrace{\sum_i U(i)}_{\text{col}(F)\text{, private}} + \underbrace{S}_{\text{ker}(F)\text{, emergent}}$$

The ratio S/I is the synergy fraction — the proportion of the swarm's information about the environment that is irreducibly collective.

The PARISI prediction: the Fisher-information-optimal synergy fraction (maximizing collective resilience against adversarial agent loss while maintaining tractable decentralized coordination) IS:

$$\frac{S^*}{I^*} = \log \varphi \approx 0.481$$

At this operating point, 48.1% of the swarm's Fisher information about the environment is synergistic — recoverable only from the global joint distribution, not from any subset. The remaining 51.9% is divided between redundancy (robust, consensus) and uniqueness (private). This IS the φ-equilibrium of the swarm's informational architecture.

The benchmark publishes computed synergy fractions; the φ-prediction is verifiable without new experiments.

## Part IV · The Ring-Attractor Consensus: Beyond Vicsek

### IV.1 Thought Experiment: The Locust That Does Not Align

Watch a marching swarm of desert locusts in the laboratory of the Konstanz Collective Behaviour Cluster. Classical Vicsek theory predicts: each locust averages the headings of its neighbours and adjusts its own heading toward that average, with some noise. Sayin, Couzin, and collaborators, using a virtual-reality setup in which each locust walks on an air-cushioned trackball surrounded by holographic neighbours under full experimental control, directly tested this postulate.

Result (*Science* 387, 995–1000, 2025): locusts do not align with neighbours' headings. When presented with a virtual neighbour whose body is rotated to face one direction while its motion trail indicates it is moving in a different direction, the real locust responds to the bearing — the direction from the real locust to the virtual neighbour's position — not to the neighbour's own heading. Locusts do not copy their neighbours' orientations. They triangulate.

The Couzin group's cognitive model: each locust maintains a neural **ring attractor** — a population of neurons arranged topologically around a ring, where activity is localized to one sector representing the current perceived consensus direction. Each nearby neighbour's bearing adds a bump of excitation to the corresponding sector of the ring. When multiple bearings compete, the ring's lateral inhibition resolves the conflict: the sector with the most input wins, its activity grows, and the locust turns toward that direction.

Ring attractors are biologically grounded. Seelig and Jayaraman (*Nature* 521, 186–191, 2015) identified the neural substrate in the *Drosophila* central complex: a physical ring of ~50 neurons whose activity encodes heading. Lyu, Abbott, and Maimon (*Nature* 601, 92–97, 2022) extended the result: the ring attractor also computes vector differences, enabling allocentric spatial computation. The ring attractor is the canonical neural consensus mechanism.

### IV.2 The Ring Attractor as the Cognitive col(F)/ker(F) Boundary

The CAJAL framework identifies the connectome as the physical col(F)/ker(F) network of biological intelligence. The PARISI framework refines this identification for swarm cognition:

**col(F) of the ring attractor**: the dominant bump — the sector of the ring with highest activity at the current moment. This is the consensus output, the observable decision, the direction the locust will take.

**ker(F) of the ring attractor**: the subthreshold activity across the rest of the ring — the bearings to neighbours whose votes did not win. This information is present in the ring but not currently expressed. It is the shadow vote, the dissenting opinion, the latent alternative.

**Boundary**: the lateral inhibition dynamics that compress activity toward the winning sector while suppressing the rest. This is the conditional-independence operator: after lateral inhibition equilibrates, the firing pattern is conditionally independent of the neighbour-bearing input history given the current dominant bump.

The ring attractor implements exact Bayesian consensus for circular variables. Its Fisher information for heading estimation saturates the von Mises Cramér–Rao bound. Evolution discovered it independently in insects (Drosophila, locusts), in rodents (head-direction cells), in bats (allocentric goal-direction cells, Sarel et al. *Science* 2017). It is the universal biological solution to the col(F)/ker(F) consensus problem on the circle S¹.

For engineered swarms: a drone implementing a ring-attractor computational primitive — rather than direct Vicsek-style averaging — gains biological robustness. The ring attractor handles conflicting neighbour information gracefully (via lateral inhibition), handles outliers (they produce bumps that fail to dominate), and naturally represents uncertainty (flat activity across the ring means low confidence). It is the Fisher-information-optimal cognitive substrate for bearings-based navigation.

The Vicsek model (1995) assumed explicit alignment. STARFLAG (2008–2010) showed that alignment propagates faster and over longer distances than Vicsek predicts, via topological interaction. The Couzin locust study (2025) showed that alignment is not even the correct microscopic rule: it is bearing-based consensus through ring-attractor dynamics, not heading copying.

The Vicsek-to-Couzin correction IS a Sherman–Morrison rank-one update to the swarm's microscopic model. The Vicsek alignment rule is replaced by the ring-attractor consensus rule. The macroscopic phenomenology — flocking, critical correlation length, scale-free dynamics — survives the correction because the relevant universality class (the Toner–Tu hydrodynamics of polar active matter, Toner & Tu 1995) depends only on symmetry and conservation laws, not on the microscopic interaction details.

But the engineering implications differ. Vicsek-based drone swarms require direct velocity sharing or imaging of neighbours' motion vectors. The Konstanz cognitive model is easier to implement than the Hungarian alignment model. And it is more robust: bearings are well-defined even when neighbours are moving erratically, whereas heading estimation from rapid angular changes is noise-amplifying.

In a spin glass — a disordered magnetic material in which the interactions between spins are random in sign and magnitude — the ground state is not a simple ferromagnetic or antiferromagnetic configuration. Giorgio Parisi, in a series of papers from 1979 to 1983, derived the replica-symmetry-breaking solution of the Sherrington–Kirkpatrick model, showing that the ultrametric structure is the signature of a specific organizational scheme for complex disordered systems.

Parisi received the Nobel Prize in Physics in 2021 for this work, shared with Syukuro Manabe and Klaus Hasselmann, "for the discovery of the interplay of disorder and fluctuations in physical systems from atomic to planetary scales."

A starling flock is a disordered system — no two flocks are alike, no two birds have identical local environments, each bird's interaction graph fluctuates as it moves. Yet collective order emerges: the flock as a whole has a well-defined direction, velocity, and shape. This is order from disorder — the same phenomenon Parisi formalized in spin glasses, now realized in bird flocks.

The STARFLAG paper (Ballerini et al., *PNAS* 2008) listed Parisi as co-author precisely because the empirical finding — topological interaction producing scale-free correlations in starling flocks — is the bird-flock manifestation of the same universal mechanism Parisi formalized in spin glasses.
